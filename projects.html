Nier:Automata™ menu in pure CSS

This project was created for learning CSS, an exercise that took two weeks. After the lesson portion of David Gray's CSS course for beginners, I decided to substitute this in place of the final project. I felt he had taught me well enough to search for exactly what else I needed to learn along the way.

Most of my time was spent going through the documentation on MDN, troubleshooting issues that either aren't well-covered or easily found on Stack Overflow and YouTube. I would like to eventually record a full course on learning CSS through the making of this site, or a series of videos of my own about the complex issues I needed to resolve throughout the process, but will save that for when I have more time and experience.

Some of the most invaluable skills I learned were how to:

  • do interactive staging and atomic conventional commits in git
  • do atomic conventional commits
  • use the browser inspector and developer tools
  • abstract properties into separate rulesets to style in different contexts without repeating the same lines
  • use containers and pseudo elements to draw visuals like gradients and shapes
  • create transitions, animations, and effects without javascript
  • assign variables to keep certain values synced across different rulesets
  • troubleshoot the interactions between flex and grid properties with their content and container properties
  • although not ideal, practice how to implement responsive design from a desktop-first approach for updating existing products

At the time of writing, the site is incomplete. Still left to do are:

  • media querying for smaller screens
  • abstracting more common properties, combining verbose properties, removing unused properties
  • polishing up margins and transition effects
  • reviewing html for accessibility

Personal Git cheatsheet in pure HTML

This project was created for practicing HTML and reviewing how to use git. After completing David Gray's HTML course for beginners, I decided to put the notes I had taken while learning Git into a basic HTML document. It features a simple navigation menu with anchor links and content using many description lists inside articles separating each category. Also has a list of references for sources and additional information, and a contact form for practice at the end.

Some of the most invaluable skills I learned were how to:

  • use basic git commands
  • use semantic tags to structure with context
  • use anchor tags and reference links

The page was intended to be combined with the next project involving CSS, as it didn't feel like I could do much more than what I could with a word doc when being limited to only HTML nothing else. I did have to at least style the code elements.

Portraits with a Salon

A few years ago, I reached out to collaborate with a local salon to help create a set of portraits. We were a team of hairsylists, makeup artists, models, and myself as photographer and editor. Over three photoshoots, we worked together to capture different variations of a light and natural vibe that would match their business' energy.

I had only worked in hospital rooms and live theaters up until then so having complete control over lighting with plenty of space for my equipment was a breath of fresh air. However, unlike other portraits I had taken before, these were beauty portraits and called for significantly more retouching. Every patch of skin needed to be unblemished, every stray hair needed to be out of the way, every skinfold and crease of clothing needed to be unassuming. AI has changed the game today but, back then, I needed techniques like layermasking and frequency separation. Over 1000 photos later, we had 10 keepers, and the work on each one took several hours. It's safe to say that post-processing took more time as the photoshoots themselves.

Some of the most invaluable skills I learned were how to:

  • simplify communication to get the desired results more efficiently
  • collaborate in a team of people with different specialties

I had always shot and edited my own images but this was probably the point I should have outsourced my editing to focus on what I did best. Regardless, it was a invaluable experience and a pleasure to work with those talented creatives. I'm very grateful to have had the chance to see it through from start to finish.

Portraits with a Ballet Theatre

Several years ago, I wanted to do dance photography. Specifically, I wanted to shoot ballet because, despite it being an art, there was such a thing as a objectively good or bad photo. After approaching several companies, I decided to work with a school whose director and manager were open to helping me select photos and develop an eye for form.

With their help, I learned that the position of a hand, the curve of a wrist, the angle of a foot, the bend of an arm or a leg -- basically every shape made with a dancer's body could make or break an otherwise beautiful photo. The performance was art in motion, but still images were mainly about proper form. It wasn't uncommon for conventionally beautiful photos to be be called garbage, or have a photo pulled from my rejects pile and be told it was a keeper. I quickly realized ballet was perhaps more skill than art in the traditional sense, and I was thrilled that it matched my pursuit of getting the perfect shot.

Some of the most invaluable skills I learned were how to:

  • propose a partnership and structure legal agreements
  • evaluate different solutions for deliverables
  • develop a streamlined workflow to expedite sales
  • run a small business

Before I left the area, I had done backstage editorials, dance rehersals, and onstage performances for Giselle and two seasons of the Nutcracker. I consider myself very lucky to have had the opportunity to work with them and their amazing students, and it was one of the most important experiences that shaped me as a photographer.
